Section 6

Contact Precaution Infections

C. difficile, MRSA, and VRE are the most NCLEX-tested contact-precaution infections. The C. diff soap-and-water rule is tested on nearly every NCLEX exam.

🚨 NCLEX Critical Safety Point: C. difficile produces SPORES that are NOT killed by alcohol-based hand sanitizer. You MUST wash hands with SOAP AND WATER when caring for C. diff patients. This single fact appears on nearly every NCLEX exam.
🟢 C. difficile (C. diff) — Contact Precautions + Soap & Water
▼
Simple Explanation: C. diff is a spore-forming bacterium that causes severe diarrhea, usually after antibiotic use disrupts the normal gut bacteria. Spores survive on surfaces for months. Alcohol hand sanitizer does NOT kill spores — soap and water is mandatory.
Transmission
  • Fecal-oral — spores survive on surfaces for months
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces or equipment
  • Healthcare worker hands (without proper washing)
  • Antibiotics disrupt normal gut flora → C. diff overgrowth
Risk Factors
  • Recent antibiotic use — ANY antibiotic (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins highest risk)
  • Hospitalization or long-term care facility
  • Age >65
  • Proton pump inhibitor (PPI) use
  • Immunocompromised state
Hallmark Symptoms
  • Profuse watery diarrhea (≥3 loose stools/day)
  • Abdominal cramping and tenderness
  • Low-grade to high fever
  • Leukocytosis — WBC may be extremely elevated (20,000–50,000+)
  • Foul-smelling stool
Diagnostics
  • Stool PCR for C. diff toxin A and B — most sensitive (preferred)
  • GDH antigen + toxin immunoassay (rapid, less sensitive)
  • CBC: leukocytosis (very high WBC is classic)
  • CT abdomen: colonic wall thickening, toxic megacolon if severe
Treatment
  • Oral vancomycin — first-line for most C. diff
  • Fidaxomicin — preferred; reduces recurrence risk
  • Metronidazole — mild cases only (less effective than vancomycin)
  • Discontinue offending antibiotic if clinically possible
  • Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) — recurrent C. diff
  • NO anti-diarrheal agents — worsen disease, increase toxin retention
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves upon room entry
  • SOAP AND WATER handwashing — NO alcohol hand sanitizer
  • Dedicated equipment stays in the room (stethoscope, BP cuff)
  • Bleach-based cleaning products for environmental decontamination
  • Monitor for dehydration — strict I&O, electrolytes
  • Monitor for toxic megacolon: abdominal distension + fever + high WBC + DECREASE in diarrhea (paradoxical sign)
Complications — Red Flags
Toxic megacolon — fever + distension + WBC >30,000 = EMERGENCY Bowel perforation → sepsis Severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance Recurrent C. diff infection (up to 25% recurrence)
Patient Teaching
  • Only take antibiotics when prescribed — never share or save them
  • Wash hands with soap and water — not hand sanitizer — after bathroom use
  • Clean bathroom surfaces with bleach solution at home
  • Probiotics may reduce risk (discuss with provider)
  • Watch for return of symptoms after treatment — C. diff frequently recurs
📘 NCLEX Pattern Recognition
A patient on day 5 of clindamycin develops profuse, watery, foul-smelling diarrhea with temperature 38.6°C and WBC 28,000. Priority actions: Apply CONTACT precautions, collect stool specimen for C. diff toxin, wash hands with SOAP AND WATER, monitor hydration and electrolytes, do NOT give anti-diarrheal agents.
🟡 MRSA — Contact Precautions, Vancomycin
▼
Simple Explanation: MRSA (Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is a common staph bacteria that has become resistant to many antibiotics including methicillin and oxacillin. It spreads through direct skin contact. Common in hospitals (HA-MRSA) and community settings (CA-MRSA).
Transmission
  • Direct contact with infected wounds or drainage
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces or equipment
  • Healthcare worker hands (most common vector)
  • HA-MRSA: invasive procedures, catheters, surgical wounds
  • CA-MRSA: skin-to-skin contact, shared equipment, crowded settings
Symptoms by Site
  • Skin/soft tissue: painful boils, abscesses, cellulitis that FAILS typical antibiotics
  • Wound infections: purulent drainage, erythema, warmth
  • Pneumonia: severe, rapid progression in hospitalized patients
  • Bacteremia/sepsis: fever, hemodynamic instability
  • Osteomyelitis, endocarditis (hematogenous spread)
Diagnostics
  • Wound culture — gram-positive cocci in clusters
  • Antibiotic sensitivity testing — resistant to methicillin/oxacillin
  • Blood cultures if systemic infection suspected
  • Nasal swab for MRSA screening/colonization on admission
Treatment
  • Vancomycin IV — first-line for serious/systemic MRSA
  • Monitor vancomycin troughs (10–20 mcg/mL; 15–20 for serious infection)
  • Linezolid, daptomycin — alternatives for serious infections
  • TMP-SMX, clindamycin — community MRSA skin infections (oral)
  • Decolonization: intranasal mupirocin + chlorhexidine body wash
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves upon room entry
  • Dedicated equipment stays in room
  • Hand hygiene before and after all care
  • Aseptic technique for all wound care
  • Monitor vancomycin levels (trough levels)
  • Assess for signs of systemic spread (sepsis)
📘 NCLEX MRSA Key Facts
MRSA wound: Contact precautions (gown + gloves). Alcohol hand sanitizer IS effective for MRSA (unlike C. diff). Vancomycin requires therapeutic drug monitoring — trough levels before 4th dose. "Red Man Syndrome" with vancomycin = slow the infusion rate and pre-medicate with diphenhydramine.
🟣 VRE — Contact Precautions, Vancomycin Resistant
▼
Simple Explanation: VRE (Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus) is enterococcal bacteria resistant to vancomycin. It can colonize patients without causing infection, but causes serious infections in immunocompromised patients. Contact precautions required. Vancomycin does NOT work.
Transmission
  • Direct contact with infected/colonized patient
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces (survives hours on surfaces)
  • Healthcare worker hands (most important vector)
  • Most common in: ICU, transplant, oncology, dialysis patients
Symptoms / Infections Caused
  • Often ASYMPTOMATIC colonization (rectal or urinary)
  • UTI, wound infection, bacteremia when symptomatic
  • Endocarditis in high-risk patients
  • Serious when occurring in immunocompromised
Treatment
  • Vancomycin does NOT work — this is the defining characteristic
  • Linezolid (first-line for most VRE infections)
  • Daptomycin (bacteremia)
  • Tigecycline (some infections)
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves on room entry
  • Dedicated equipment in room — never share between patients
  • Enhanced environmental cleaning (bleach or EPA-approved disinfectant)
  • Hand hygiene with soap and water OR alcohol rub (both effective for VRE)
  • Educate patient and family about isolation and why it is needed
📘 NCLEX VRE Key
A patient with VRE is ordered vancomycin for another infection. The nurse should QUESTION this order — VRE is by definition vancomycin-resistant. Linezolid is the appropriate alternative. Always verify the organism's sensitivity pattern before administering antibiotics.

🖐️ Hand Hygiene Rules — The Critical Differences

❌ Alcohol Hand Sanitizer DOES NOT WORK for C. diff
C. difficile forms hardy SPORES that alcohol cannot penetrate or destroy. Using only hand sanitizer when caring for C. diff patients allows spore transmission and spreads infection to other patients. SOAP AND WATER is mandatory — the physical washing action removes spores from hands.
✅ When to Use Soap and Water vs Alcohol Rub
  • SOAP AND WATER required: C. diff, norovirus, when hands are visibly soiled, after restroom use
  • Alcohol hand rub preferred: MRSA, VRE, most other pathogens when hands are not visibly soiled
  • Either is acceptable: Most standard precaution situations
  • Remember: Soap and water is NEVER wrong — it is always appropriate

Section 7

Sepsis & Systemic Infection

Sepsis is a life-threatening emergency. Early recognition and immediate intervention — especially antibiotics and fluids — are the most important nursing actions.

🚨 NCLEX Emergency Pattern: Fever OR hypothermia + tachycardia + tachypnea + altered mental status + hypotension = SEPSIS until proven otherwise. Draw blood cultures, start broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour, and give IV fluid bolus. Every hour of delay increases mortality.
⚠️ Sepsis — Complete Clinical Reference
▼
Simple Explanation: Sepsis is when an infection triggers a chain reaction throughout the entire body, damaging organs. It progresses rapidly from manageable to life-threatening. The nurse's ability to recognize early signs and act immediately saves lives.
Pathophysiology (Simplified)
  • Infection → immune system overreacts → widespread inflammation
  • Vasodilation → ↓vascular resistance → hypotension
  • Capillary leak → fluid shifts → further hypoperfusion
  • Microvascular clotting → organ ischemia → organ dysfunction
  • End result: multi-organ failure if untreated
Early Signs of Sepsis (Act Now)
  • Temperature >38°C (100.4°F) OR <36°C (96.8°F) — hypothermia is ominous
  • Heart rate >90 bpm (tachycardia)
  • Respiratory rate >20 breaths/min
  • WBC >12,000 or <4,000 or >10% bands (immature neutrophils)
  • Altered mental status (confusion, restlessness, new agitation)
  • Elevated blood glucose without diabetes
Late Signs — Organ Failure (Critical)
  • Hypotension: SBP <90 mmHg or MAP <65 mmHg
  • Oliguria: urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 2+ hours
  • Elevated lactate: >2 mmol/L = tissue hypoperfusion
  • Rising creatinine, elevated bilirubin (organ dysfunction)
  • Coagulation abnormalities (DIC risk)
  • Mottled, cyanotic extremities
qSOFA Score (Bedside Screening Tool)
Score 1 point each:
• Altered mental status (GCS <15)
• Respiratory rate ≥22 breaths/min
• Systolic BP ≤100 mmHg
Score ≥2 = high suspicion for sepsis — escalate immediately
Sepsis Hour-1 Bundle (Priority Actions)
  • 1. Measure lactate level — lactate ≥4 = septic shock territory
  • 2. Blood cultures × 2 before antibiotics (from 2 different sites)
  • 3. Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour — do NOT delay
  • 4. IV fluid bolus: 30 mL/kg crystalloid (NS or LR) if hypotensive or lactate ≥4
  • 5. Vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) if MAP <65 after fluid resuscitation
  • Repeat lactate if initial >2 mmol/L
Ongoing Monitoring
  • Strict hourly urine output (goal: >0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Continuous vital signs monitoring
  • Serial lactate levels (should improve with treatment)
  • Frequent neuro checks (mental status)
  • Skin color, temperature, capillary refill
  • Response to fluid resuscitation

📘 NCLEX Critical Reasoning — Cultures Before Antibiotics
Blood cultures should be drawn BEFORE antibiotics if it can be done without significantly delaying treatment. In a critically ill patient, antibiotics MUST be given within 1 hour — do not delay antibiotics more than 45 minutes to get cultures. The NCLEX tests this order: cultures → antibiotics ASAP (both within the 1-hour window when possible).

⏱️ Sepsis Bundle — Nursing Priority Actions

RECOGNIZE IT EARLY
  • Fever OR hypothermia
  • Tachycardia (>90 bpm)
  • Tachypnea (>20/min)
  • Altered mental status
  • qSOFA ≥2 points
  • Elevated WBC or bands
ACT WITHIN 1 HOUR
  • Notify provider STAT
  • Draw lactate level
  • Blood cultures × 2 (before abx)
  • Start IV antibiotics
  • 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid if hypotensive
  • Large-bore IV access × 2
MONITOR CONTINUOUSLY
  • Urine output q1h (goal >30 mL/hr)
  • Serial vital signs
  • Repeat lactate (should decrease)
  • Mental status checks
  • Skin perfusion assessment
  • Response to interventions
