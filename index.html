Section 6

Contact Precaution Infections

C. difficile, MRSA, and VRE require contact precautions. The C. diff soap-and-water rule is one of the most tested facts on every NCLEX exam.

🚨 NCLEX Critical Safety Point: C. difficile produces SPORES that are NOT killed by alcohol-based hand sanitizer. SOAP AND WATER is mandatory. This appears on nearly every NCLEX exam.
🟢 C. difficile — Contact Precautions + Soap & Water Only
▼
Simple Explanation: C. diff is a spore-forming bacterium causing severe diarrhea, usually after antibiotics disrupt normal gut flora. Spores survive on surfaces for months. Alcohol hand sanitizer does NOT kill spores — soap and water is the only effective hand hygiene method.
Transmission & Risk Factors
  • Fecal-oral — spores survive on surfaces for months
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces or equipment
  • Healthcare worker hands (without proper soap washing)
  • Recent antibiotic use — ANY antibiotic (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins = highest risk)
  • Hospitalization or long-term care residence
  • Age >65, PPI use, immunocompromised
Hallmark Symptoms
  • Profuse watery, foul-smelling diarrhea (≥3 stools/day)
  • Abdominal cramping and tenderness
  • Low-grade to high fever
  • Leukocytosis — WBC may be very high (20,000–50,000+)
Diagnostics
  • Stool PCR for C. diff toxin A and B — most sensitive (preferred)
  • CBC: leukocytosis (classic and sometimes extreme)
  • CT abdomen if toxic megacolon suspected
Treatment
  • Oral vancomycin — first-line for most C. diff
  • Fidaxomicin — preferred; reduces recurrence
  • Metronidazole — mild cases only (less effective)
  • Discontinue the offending antibiotic if possible
  • Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) for recurrent C. diff
  • NO anti-diarrheal agents — traps toxins, risks toxic megacolon
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves upon room entry
  • SOAP AND WATER handwashing — NO alcohol hand rub
  • Dedicated equipment stays in the room
  • Bleach-based cleaning products for decontamination
  • Monitor for dehydration — strict I&O, electrolytes
  • Watch for toxic megacolon: distension + fever + sudden decrease in diarrhea
Complications: Toxic megacolon (WBC >30,000 + distension + sudden decrease in diarrhea = EMERGENCY), bowel perforation, sepsis, recurrent infection (25% recurrence rate).
Patient Teaching
  • Only take antibiotics when prescribed — never share or save them
  • Wash hands with soap and water after bathroom use — not hand sanitizer
  • Clean bathroom surfaces at home with bleach solution
  • Watch for return of symptoms after treatment — C. diff frequently recurs
📘 NCLEX Pattern Recognition
Patient on day 5 of clindamycin: profuse watery foul-smelling diarrhea, T 38.6°C, WBC 28,000. First priority: Apply CONTACT precautions + wash hands with SOAP AND WATER + collect stool for C. diff toxin. Do NOT give loperamide.
🟡 MRSA — Contact Precautions, Vancomycin
▼
Simple Explanation: MRSA (Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is a staph bacteria resistant to many antibiotics. It spreads by direct skin contact. Common in hospitals (HA-MRSA) and communities (CA-MRSA).
Transmission
  • Direct contact with infected wounds or drainage
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces or equipment
  • Healthcare worker hands (most common vector in hospitals)
Symptoms by Site
  • Skin/soft tissue: painful boils, abscesses, cellulitis failing typical antibiotics
  • Wound infections: purulent drainage, erythema, warmth
  • HA-MRSA: pneumonia, bacteremia, surgical site infections
  • Sepsis if systemic spread occurs
Diagnostics
  • Wound culture — gram-positive cocci in clusters, resistant to methicillin
  • Blood cultures if systemic infection suspected
  • Nasal swab for admission MRSA screening
Treatment
  • Vancomycin IV — first-line for serious/systemic MRSA
  • Monitor vancomycin troughs (15–20 mcg/mL for serious infection)
  • Linezolid, daptomycin — alternatives
  • TMP-SMX or clindamycin — community MRSA skin infections (oral)
  • Decolonization: intranasal mupirocin + chlorhexidine baths
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves upon room entry
  • Dedicated equipment stays in room
  • Aseptic technique for all wound care
  • Monitor vancomycin trough levels
  • Assess for signs of systemic spread (sepsis)
Note: Alcohol hand rub IS effective for MRSA (unlike C. diff). Either soap and water OR alcohol hand rub is acceptable for MRSA.
📘 NCLEX Key: Vancomycin Red Man Syndrome
Red Man Syndrome = flushing, erythema, pruritus from too-rapid vancomycin infusion. This is NOT a true allergy. Action: SLOW the infusion rate (infuse over at least 60 minutes). Pre-medicate with diphenhydramine for future doses if needed.
🟣 VRE — Contact Precautions, Vancomycin Does NOT Work
▼
Simple Explanation: VRE (Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus) is enterococcal bacteria resistant to vancomycin. Often causes asymptomatic colonization but can cause serious infections in immunocompromised patients. Contact precautions required. Vancomycin is ineffective by definition.
Transmission & Risk
  • Direct contact with infected or colonized patient
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces (survives hours on surfaces)
  • Healthcare worker hands
  • Most common in: ICU, oncology, transplant, dialysis patients
Symptoms
  • Often ASYMPTOMATIC colonization (rectal or urinary)
  • When symptomatic: UTI, wound infection, bacteremia
  • Serious infections in immunocompromised patients
Treatment
  • Vancomycin DOES NOT work — defining characteristic of VRE
  • Linezolid — first-line for most VRE infections
  • Daptomycin — bacteremia and endocarditis
  • Tigecycline — some infections
Priority Nursing Actions
  • CONTACT precautions — gown and gloves on room entry
  • Dedicated equipment in room — never share between patients
  • Enhanced environmental cleaning
  • Either soap/water or alcohol hand rub (both effective for VRE)
  • Educate patient about isolation — explain it is protective
📘 NCLEX VRE Safety
A patient with VRE is ordered vancomycin for a separate infection. The nurse should QUESTION this order — VRE is by definition vancomycin-resistant. Linezolid is the appropriate alternative. Always verify organism sensitivity before administering antibiotics.

🖐️ Hand Hygiene — Critical Differences

❌ Alcohol Hand Sanitizer DOES NOT Work for C. diff
C. difficile forms SPORES that alcohol cannot penetrate. Soap and water is mandatory — the physical washing action mechanically removes spores from hands.
✅ When to Use What
  • SOAP AND WATER required: C. diff, norovirus, visibly soiled hands
  • Either is fine: MRSA, VRE, most standard precaution situations
  • Soap and water is NEVER wrong — always appropriate

Sepsis & Systemic Infection

Sepsis is a life-threatening emergency. Early recognition and immediate intervention — antibiotics and fluids — are the most critical nursing actions.

🚨 NCLEX Emergency Pattern: Fever OR hypothermia + tachycardia + tachypnea + altered mental status + hypotension = SEPSIS. Blood cultures → antibiotics within 1 hour → 30 mL/kg IV fluids. Every hour of delay increases mortality.
⚠️ Sepsis — Complete Clinical Reference
▼
Simple Explanation: Sepsis is when an infection triggers a massive chain reaction throughout the body, damaging organs. It progresses rapidly from manageable to fatal. The nurse's ability to recognize early signs and act immediately saves lives.
Early Signs of Sepsis — Act Now
  • Temperature >38°C (100.4°F) OR <36°C (96.8°F) — hypothermia is ominous
  • Heart rate >90 bpm (tachycardia)
  • Respiratory rate >20 breaths/min (tachypnea)
  • WBC >12,000 or <4,000 or >10% bands
  • New altered mental status (confusion, restlessness, agitation)
Late Signs — Organ Failure (Critical)
  • Hypotension: SBP <90 mmHg or MAP <65 mmHg
  • Oliguria: urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 2+ hours
  • Elevated lactate: >2 mmol/L = tissue hypoperfusion
  • Rising creatinine (renal dysfunction)
  • Elevated bilirubin (hepatic dysfunction)
  • Mottled, cyanotic skin
qSOFA Bedside Screening Tool
Score 1 point each:
• Altered mental status (GCS <15)
• Respiratory rate ≥22 breaths/min
• Systolic BP ≤100 mmHg
Score ≥2 = high suspicion for sepsis — escalate immediately
Sepsis Hour-1 Bundle — Priority Actions
  • 1. Measure lactate level — ≥4 mmol/L = septic shock
  • 2. Blood cultures × 2 — from 2 different sites BEFORE antibiotics
  • 3. IV broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour
  • 4. 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid (NS or LR) if hypotensive or lactate ≥4
  • 5. Vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) if MAP <65 after fluids
Ongoing Monitoring
  • Strict hourly urine output (goal: >0.5 mL/kg/hr or >30 mL/hr)
  • Continuous vital signs monitoring
  • Serial lactate levels (should decrease with treatment)
  • Frequent neuro checks (GCS, mental status)
  • Skin color, temperature, capillary refill
High-Risk Patients: Elderly, immunocompromised (HIV, chemo, steroids), diabetes, indwelling catheters/IV lines, post-surgical, chronic kidney or liver disease.

📘 NCLEX Critical Point — Cultures Before Antibiotics
Blood cultures should be drawn BEFORE antibiotics to identify the organism. However, in a critically ill septic patient, do NOT delay antibiotics more than 45 minutes to obtain cultures. The rule: cultures → antibiotics → both within the 1-hour window. When in doubt, antibiotics come first.

⏱️ Sepsis Response — Three Priorities

RECOGNIZE EARLY
  • Fever OR hypothermia
  • Tachycardia (>90 bpm)
  • Tachypnea (>20/min)
  • New altered mental status
  • qSOFA score ≥2
ACT WITHIN 1 HOUR
  • Notify provider STAT
  • Draw lactate level
  • Blood cultures × 2
  • IV antibiotics STAT
  • 30 mL/kg IV fluid if hypotensive
  • Large-bore IV × 2
MONITOR CONTINUOUSLY
  • Urine output q1h (>30 mL/hr)
  • Serial vital signs
  • Repeat lactate level
  • Mental status checks
  • Skin perfusion
  • Response to interventions
